01 · About

How I got here

In high school, I became fascinated by color — not just as aesthetics, but as a question: where does it come from? That led me to spectroscopy, where I first saw how matter encodes information in the way it interacts with light. Around the same time, an environmental science class introduced me to systems thinking — the idea that everything is connected, and that those connections leave traces.

Those two instincts brought me to Earth and Environmental Sciences at Seoul National University. Then, in a course on Earth system science, I encountered isotope fractionation — and recognized something familiar. The same logic as IR spectroscopy: matter responding to its environment, leaving a readable signal. Except here, the recorder is the Earth itself, and the timescales are geological.

That was the moment I knew I wanted to work with stable isotopes. Since then, I've taken nearly every isotope course my department offers, and found my way into volatile geochemistry — getting my hands on a vacuum line for the first time and realizing this is a field where I can work across different proxies, instruments, and timescales, talking to people who ask questions about the whole Earth.

I'm currently in the combined B.S.–M.S. program at SNU (expected 2028), looking to pursue a Ph.D. in stable isotope geochemistry — wherever the most interesting volatile research is happening.

02 · Research

What I'm thinking about

Stable Isotope & Volatile Geochemistry (안정동위원소·휘발성 원소 지구화학)

My methodological home. I'm currently working on nitrogen budgets and elemental recycling — tracing where elements come from, where they go, and how they're partitioned across the Earth system.

Early Earth & Redox Evolution (초기 지구·산화환원 진화)

I'm drawn to the question of how the early Earth came to be the way it is — particularly its oxygen fugacity and elemental budgets. Less "how things flow," more "why the system settled into this state."

Spectroscopy × Isotopes (분광학과 동위원소의 접점)

Spectroscopy was my first scientific love, and I see isotope fractionation as the same logic written at a different scale. I'm interested in where these two ways of reading matter meet.
